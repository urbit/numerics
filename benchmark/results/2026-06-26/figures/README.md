# Figures (Tufte style)

Generated by `tools/bench_plots.py` from `bench-timing-all.csv` (32-bit, per-call
ns, baseline-subtracted, mean over sets 1–4).  PNG for viewing, PDF for print.
Design: log scale (data spans 7 orders of magnitude), range frames, minimal ink,
direct labels, small multiples.

### fig1 — `fig1-rd-dotplot`
Cleveland dot plot, all 15 transcendentals on **@rd (f64)**.  Each row: jetted (●),
Chebyshev-interpreted (○), Taylor-interpreted (|), joined by a guide line.  Reading:
the **jetted column is uniformly ~2 µs** (left), while interpreted Chebyshev sits
1–2 decades right and Taylor 2–5 decades right — and the per-arm *spread* of Taylor
(e.g. `asin` at 2×10⁸ ns, `sqt` at 7×10⁴) is the iterative method's Achilles' heel.

### fig2 — `fig2-doors-exp`
Small multiples: `exp` jetted (●) → Chebyshev-interpreted (○) for each precision
(@rd/@rs/@rh/@rq).  Shows the **jet speedup is uniform across precisions** (177×,
185×, 192×, 130×) — the slope is essentially constant, confirming the jet helps f16
and f128 as much as f64.

### fig3 — `fig3-accuracy`
Dot plot of **Taylor's relative error** vs the accurate Chebyshev (=jet, ≤1 ULP,
dashed reference).  `sqt` sits on the 1-ULP line (tie); most arms are ~1e-9..1e-10
(Taylor loses ~half the f64 digits); `sin`/`cos`/`tan` are **catastrophic** (~10³ —
no valid digits, from alternating-series cancellation).  The jet is faster *and*
more accurate.
